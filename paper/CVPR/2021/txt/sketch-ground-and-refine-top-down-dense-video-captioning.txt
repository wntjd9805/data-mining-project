Abstract
The dense video captioning task aims to detect and de-scribe a sequence of events in a video for detailed and co-herent storytelling. Previous works mainly adopt a â€œdetect-then-describeâ€ framework, which ï¬rstly detects event pro-posals in the video and then generates descriptions for the detected events. However, the deï¬nitions of events are di-verse which could be as simple as a single action or as com-plex as a set of events, depending on different semantic con-texts. Therefore, directly detecting events based on video information is ill-deï¬ned and hurts the coherency and accu-racy of generated dense captions. In this work, we reverse the predominant â€œdetect-then-describeâ€ fashion, proposing a top-down way to ï¬rst generate paragraphs from a global view and then ground each event description to a video seg-ment for detailed reï¬nement. It is formulated as a Sketch,
Ground, and Reï¬ne process (SGR). The sketch stage ï¬rst generates a coarse-grained multi-sentence paragraph to de-scribe the whole video, where each sentence is treated as an event and gets localised in the grounding stage. In the re-ï¬ning stage, we improve captioning quality via reï¬nement-enhanced training and dual-path cross attention on both coarse-grained event captions and aligned event segments.
The updated event caption can further adjust its segment boundaries. Our SGR model outperforms state-of-the-art methods on ActivityNet Captioning benchmark under tradi-tional and story-oriented dense caption evaluations. Code will be released at github.com/bearcatt/SGR. 1.

Dense Video Captioning (DVC) [11] aims to discover a se-quence of key events in a video and describe them using a coherent story. Therefore, DVC can beneï¬t many real-world applications such as content-based video retrieval and recommendation, and has become an important task in language-based video understanding research.
DVC is a challenging task since the generated event cap-tions are expected to be correct, concise, and coherent in the context of a video-level story, so as to support video under-standing. Most existing methods [11, 13, 33, 43] in this ï¬eld adopt a common â€œdetect-then-describeâ€ framework which solves the problem from a straightforward bottom-up per-spective, i.e., ï¬rst detect a large set of event proposals (even up to 103), then caption each proposal to obtain dense de-scriptions. However, one obvious issue of this framework is that the event proposals are independently generated with-out considering their temporal correlations, thus making the resultant event captions highly redundant and incoherent.

In this paper, we reverse the predominant â€œdetect-then-describeâ€ fashion and propose to solve DVC from a top-down perspective, i.e., generating a video-level story at ï¬rst and then ground each sentence in the story to video segment for detailed reï¬nement. By doing this, the event segments are predicted not only based on the visual information, but also the semantic coherence from the text. Thus the afore-mentioned issues are waived. Our model is formulated as a
Sketch, Ground, and Reï¬ne (SGR) procedure.
In the Sketch stage, we focus on the structure coherency of the event captions. We ï¬rst leverage the entire video in-formation to generate a coherent video-level paragraph so as to describe the video from a global perspective. Note that, the sentences in this paragraph naturally deï¬nes a se-quence of story-oriented events in the video. Therefore, it is not necessary for our framework to generate a large number of event proposals as in bottom-up approaches. In fact, our top-down SGR contains no event proposal generation pro-cess. Instead, we use a video Grounding module to localise the sentences (i.e., events) in our video-level paragraph.
The sentences generated so far may lack some ï¬ne-grained details since we did not explicitly consider their event-speciï¬c information. Thus, in the Reï¬ne stage, we focus on the ï¬ne-grained details of the event captions.
Speciï¬cally, we propose a Dual-Path Cross Attention mod-ule to dynamically focus on the event-level information and the coarse-grained sentence. A reï¬nement-enhanced train-ing scheme is also designed to facilitate the reï¬nement.
Based on the reï¬ned descriptions, we further adjust their event segments by feeding them into the grounding module again. We illustrate the difference between our SGR and the previous DVC frameworks in Figure 1.
